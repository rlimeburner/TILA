Welcome to TILA

The Tuckernuck Island Landowners Association (T.I.L.A.) is dedicated to preserving the natural beauty, heritage, and community spirit of Tuckernuck Island. We represent the interests of landowners, promote sustainable stewardship, and foster collaboration among residents to ensure the island’s future is protected due to its unique environment.

About

The Tuckernuck Island Landowners Association (TILA) is a community of dedicated landowners who share a commitment to preserving the natural beauty, ecological integrity, and selective development of Tuckernuck Island. Our association fosters a spirit of cooperation, transparency, and responsible stewardship among members, working collectively to address the needs and aspirations of our unique island community. Through active engagement, strategic planning, and respect for both tradition and innovation, TILA strives to ensure the long-term health and vitality of Tuckernuck Island for present and future generations."

Mission

Our mission is to protect the island’s resources, celebrate its cultural history, support initiatives to enhance the quality of life on the island amongst all landowners to coexist with nature. Presently TILA's focus is more homeowner oriented, with an emphasis on Tuckernuckers’ safety and health, landowner communication, and preserving and enjoying the island. An annual meeting of the Tuckernuck Island Landowners Association is usually held at the Firehouse on the 1st Sunday in July in combination with the Tuckernuck Firefighter’s Association (TFA). An elected TILA Executive Committee meets annually in July to discuss such issues as boat access to limited docks, road repair, erosion, health issues due to ticks and mosquitoes, natural resources, etc.

    Beverly Dammin has written a local island cookbook called WHAT THE YOHO EATS featuring recipes, personal anecdotes, and historical notes related to Tuckernuck Island and its community. The book is beautifully illustrated by her son Tucker. Many favorite, albeit unusual, recipes of landowners, past and present, are described in detail. The cookbook benefits the Tuckernuck Firefighters Association and is dedicated to Alfred Byron Coffin Jr., founder of the Tuckernuck Firefighters Association.

    A few Recipes:
    Nantucket Firemen's Supper: Includes thick pork chops, potatoes, and onions baked in milk.
    Baking Powder Biscuits: Simple recipe for fluffy biscuits using flour, baking powder, and milk.
    Byron's Eels: Fried eels coated in flour and salt.
    Bluefish Pate: Poached bluefish mixed with herbs and spices, avoiding food processors for texture.
    Wedding Chowder: A communal dish made with quohogs, potatoes, and canned milk, ideal for gatherings.

    Seafood Dishes
    Baked Bass with Egg Chive Sauce: Stuffed bass baked with vegetables and served with a creamy sauce.
    Maya's Lobster Risotto: Lobster broth-based risotto with fresh herbs and optional lobster meat.
    Sea Clam Pie: Clams and potatoes baked with a Bisquick crust.
    Clam's Casino: Fresh clams topped with breadcrumbs and garlic butter, baked until crispy.

    Soups and Chowders
    Improviser's Bean Soup: A flexible recipe using leftover beans and vegetables.
    Chowder by Bam Lafarge: Quahogs, bacon, and potatoes cooked in milk or cream.
    Lobster Chowder: Rich chowder made with lobster juice and milk.

    Breads and Breakfast
    Baking Powder Biscuits: Quick and easy biscuits for breakfast.
    Foccaccia: Pizza dough topped with olive oil and herbs.

    Desserts
    ABC Coffee Cake: A simple coffee cake recipe with streusel topping.
    Nini's Any Berry Tart: Tart crust filled with custard and topped with fresh berries.
    Hot Molasses Cake: A moist cake made with molasses and spices.

    Richard Limeburner wrote in 2025 a multi-visual History of the Tuckernuck Lagoon from 1661 to 2025 that shows mostly old charts of the Nantucket region drawn by historic European navigators, supplemented with more recent aerial, satellite, and personal photos and videos. The Lagoon has opened and closed a few times in the past to become South Pond (Tuckernuck, Ed Coffin, 1891 - 1929, pg 2 and 180) and recently closed again in March 2025. The Lagoon was the landowners primary protected mooring area from the 1960's until 2025 when it rapidly closed off due to migrating sand from the south. The Lagoon is now called South Pond as it was in the past. This collection of old charts and recent satellite images can be found at

    Membership & Dues

    To become a member of the Tuckernuck Island Landowners Association you must be a property landowner. Membership connects you with a community of neighbors committed to protecting and enhancing the island’s unique way of life. As a member, you’ll have a voice in key decisions, access to important updates, and opportunities to participate in meaningful initiatives.

    • Representation in land use and conservation issues
    • Voting rights in annual meetings
    • Access to member-only news and events
    • Eligibility to serve on committees and the board

    ACCESS 2025 - Since the lagoon closed during March 2025 seasonal moorings have been moved to various locations along the north shore. A private dock was installed May 2025 just NW of Great Rock on land owned by the Hanson family.

    2025 Hanson dock

    ACCESS 2025 - TILA does not plan to deploy the TILA dock at Brooks Landing during summer 2025. Presently there are 16 moorings at Brooks Landing. The LaFarge family allows landowners walking access over their land to boat moorings, but no vehicle parking will be allowed. Dropping off and picking up gear by vehicle will be allowed on the existing road, but no driving vehicles past Bam's boathouse nor over the dunes to the beach is permitted. Please be aware that during a Northeast Storm no boats should be moored at Brooks Landing.

    Brooks Landing June 2025

    ATV Use during 2025 - Many landowners commented during July and August 2025 about the number of ATVs on Tuckernuck this summer, the noise associated with their use, and how many ATV'd were speeding. We suggest that driving any vehicle with excessive speed is dangerous, illegal and strongly discouraged on Tuckernuck. Note, there are reasonable state laws for youth under 18 to operate ATVs responsibly. The state of Massachusetts requires that ATV operators must be 18 years of age or older, unless they have completed one mandatory session of an approved recreation vehicle safety and responsibility course. They must do this in the company of a parent or guardian. Those completing this session may operate an ATV at 16 and 17 years of age. Fourteen through 16-year-olds may operate an ATV with an engine capacity of 90 cubic centimeters or less.

    • Tuckernuck Tides During 2013 R. Limeburner measured the sea surface variability with very precise pressure recorders, courtesy of the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution (WHOI) at 8 locations around Tuckernuck. The semi-diurnal (12.4 hour) tide dominates the local movement of the sea surface around Tuckernuck. The wind speed and direction also have a strong influence on sea level, especially during storms with breaking waves when large amounts of sand are transported by strong currents. This results in extensive erosion and acretion of the shoreline in different areas. See
